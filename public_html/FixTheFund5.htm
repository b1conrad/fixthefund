Statement of Purpose


To inform interested parties about the Victim Compensation Fund, what it is, why Congress created it, why it is unfair, the ulterior motive behind the inequities, and how to fix it.


 


What is the Victim Compensation Fund?


The Victim Compensation Fund is part of the Airline Bailout Package. Days after the Sept. 11 attacks when approximately 3,000 people were murdered, the airlines asked Congress for financial help (because the airspace was shut down) and legal protection from what they expected would be a flood of lawsuits. Congress gave the airlines $10 billion in loans, $5 billion in grants, and restricted their liability for damages arising from the attacks to the limits of insurance liability coverage maintained by the air carrier. Later, other entities, including the City of New York, the Port Authority of New York and New Jersey, and Larry Silverstein, the World Trade Center leaseholder, were given the same protection.


Lawsuits were expected, and indeed, have been filed. Because of the large dollar amounts claimed from Workers’ Compensation insurers, property owners and others, experts and lawmakers knew there would be little to nothing left for the families of the Victims if they joined in a lawsuit. So, considering the help and protection given the airlines and others, Congress created the Victim Compensation Fund to compensate the Families for their losses.


The crucial point is the Victim Compensation Fund was set up to give the Victims' Families an alternative to litigation that would provide them what they would have gotten from a successful lawsuit arising from their loved ones being murdered.


The Victim Compensation Fund is NOT a charity. It is a separate federal government program, set up as an alternative to litigation. Eligible individuals who wish to participate must sign away their right to sue the airlines, the Port Authority (owner of the WTC) the City of New York, security agencies, etc.




Why is the Victim Compensation Fund unfair?


1.         The Victim Compensation Fund punishes people who decided to plan to take care of their family.


A.        If the victim bought life insurance, those proceeds are subtracted from the computed award. 


B.        If the victim had a pension, that pension is subtracted from the computed award. 


C.        A widow(er) with children will get less money from the Fund than if they didn't have children because the Social Security payments going to the children are deducted from the computed award.


These points, written into the law, don’t happen in court. They do not subtract life insurance proceeds, pensions and government payments from damages awarded in a lawsuit. Consider this:


Mr. “D” chose to take part of his salary and paid for life insurance rather than buy that new car that he needed. When he was killed on 9/11, he “owned” his life insurance policy.


Mr. “T” chose to take part of his salary and bought that new car he needed. When he was killed on 9/11, he “owned” his new car.


Under the rules of the Victim Compensation Fund, Mr. “D’s” family must deduct his insurance policy amount from their award from the Victim Compensation Fund total. Mr. “T’s” family gets to keep his new car. Is this fair?


9/11 Victims who planned ahead and tried to do the responsible thing for their family’s future are being penalized for their sacrifice and forethought.


Only Congress can correct these inequities in the Victim Compensation Fund.


2.         In its implementation of The Victim Compensation Fund, the government, in the person of Mr. Kenneth Feinberg, the administrator or “Special Master” of the Fund, is basing the $250,000 presumed “non-economic” award on three existing, but old, federal laws rather than using contemporary court precedents. These laws are, the amount of life insurance for military personnel killed in the line of duty, the death benefit amount for Public Safety Officers killed in the line of duty, and the amount paid by the government for government-administered vaccine-related deaths or injury.


This sounds reasonable until you learn that the $250,000 figure is based on a 1975 California law that capped non-economic damages in medical malpractice lawsuits and has never been adjusted for inflation.


To understand how an “award” is computed, two factors are used:


            a.         “Economic losses” are computed by figuring how much money the victim would have earned over the rest of his/her working life, then subtracting what it would have cost that person to live. What is left over is called the “economic loss.”
 


            b.         “Non-economic losses” means losses for physical and emotional pain, suffering, physical impairment, mental anguish, disfigurement, loss of companionship, loss of consortium loss of enjoyment of life, hedonic damages, etc. Mr. Feinberg set the presumed “non-economic losses” at a flat rate of $250,000 for the victim and $100,000 for a spouse and $100,000 for each child. (But remember, the Social Security deduction, mentioned earlier.) The $250,000 figure is far below what is customarily awarded in court, (as this is an alternative to litigation) and represents 1975 standards.


Congressional testimony stated that this Act would give the Victims of the September 11 attacks full compensation for their losses. As it stands, it does not. The Victims’ Families deserve the same rights as every other American citizen. If, in the national interest, airlines and others needed to be protected, thus restricting our individual rights to sue, then the substitute compensation program must use the same formulas that would be used in court and not figures based on a 26-year-old law.


We must emphatically say that the $250,000 figure is the presumed non-economic award. Mr. Kenneth Feinberg, the Special Master, said that in six of the first fourteen cases awarded, the non-economic award was larger than $250,000. However, it presumably requires the family to opt to have a hearing if they choose the “Track A” processing track or use “Track B” which starts with a hearing, and we believe would require the family to show extraordinary circumstances


  


What is the Ulterior Motive?


Besides being unfair, there is an ulterior motive at work here. There are forces in big business that want the $250,000 non-economic loss figure and the deduction of insurance, etc., to be a reality in all tort cases. This is called “tort reform” and one of the things they want are strict limitations on non-economic damage awards. Why else would a 27-year-old monetary figure that has never been updated for inflation keep getting inserted into laws and used in regulations (like the VCF)?


The tort system (which means a personal injury or wrongful-death lawsuit) is the only way we citizens have of punishing large corporations when a company makes a defective product that injures or kills somebody. Regulations were supposed to do the job, but big business gets away with a lot because their lobbyists are so strong.


For example, take the October 29, 2002 New York Times story on . . . “a Graphic Look at Towers’ Fall.” This “trove” of material, including videotape, CD’s, mathematical analyses and high-tech imagery, represents the largest single repository of raw data and expert analysis on the WTC attack, dwarfing the analysis compiled by the government in its first look of how and why the towers collapsed. This is all part of a $3.5 billion lawsuit and would not have been done, had a lawsuit not been at stake.


The tort system is how big companies are brought to accountability when their products are defective. For example, look at Ford and Firestone. Would they have budged an inch had big money not been involved? This is why big business wants the “pain and suffering” damage potential to be limited.


Note, here, we are not talking about the excessively high awards, rarely made, but widely reported by the press. Those awards are usually always immediately reduced by the judge, or later, on appeal. What we are talking about, for example, is the child that got thrown from a defective car seat and is injured for life. Money is the only enforcement method to punish a large corporation, and if you limit that possible punishment, you take away the enforcement tool. It would be like reducing the sentence for murder to five years. How much of a deterrent would that be?


Now, perhaps you understand why the Families are asking that the $250,000 figure be adjusted for inflation since 1975, which would make it close to $1,000,000. This will help keep big business from having an ever-reducing (in real dollars) reduction in penalties for harmful or fatal defective products.


The same applies to the subtractions for insurance, etc. It is against public policy to use any financial asset that the victim already has to reduce what the defendant owes. Life insurance is a financial asset. Since Congress obligated the government to stand in for the defendant, they should not subtract life insurance from what the government owes the Victims’ Families.




Fix the Fund's Goal


To have Congress pass, and the President sign, House bill HR 3665 which:


1.         Repeals the section of the original law that specifies the deduction of insurance, etc.


2.         Specifies the minimum amount for non-economic damages at the inflation-adjusted figure of $1,000,000 per family.
