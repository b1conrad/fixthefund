Fix the Fund



Statement of Purpose


To inform the people of the United States and the Congress about the Victim Compensation Fund, what it is, why Congress created it, why it is unfair, the ulterior motive behind the inequities, and how to fix it.

 

What is the Victim Compensation Fund?


The Victim Compensation Fund is part of the Airline Bailout Package. To protect American Airlines and United Airlines (whose planes were highjacked) from being sued out of existence by the Victims’ Families, Congress limited their financial liability to the amount of insurance they carried. With little to no money to be gained from suing, Congress effectively took away the Victims’ Families right to sue.


The Victim Compensation Fund was set up to give the Victims’ Families what we would have gotten from a successful court proceeding.


The Victim Compensation Fund is NOT a charity. It is a government program ostensibly set up to restore the rights of the Victims’ Families to seek restitution for a crime committed against them. This right was, for all practical purposes, taken away from the Victims by limiting the airlines’ liability.



Why is the Victim Compensation Fund unfair?

 

1.   The Victim Compensation Fund punishes people who decided to plan to take care of their family.

 

             A.    If the victim bought life insurance, those proceeds are subtracted from the computed award.

             B.    If the victim had a pension, that pension is subtracted from the computed award.

             C.    A widow(er) with children will get less money from the Fund than if they didn’t have children because the Social Security payments going to the children are deducted from the computed award.

 

These points, written into the law, never happen in court. Life insurance proceeds, pensions and government payments are not subtracted from damages awarded in a lawsuit.

 

2.   In its implementation of The Victim Compensation Fund, the administration, in the person of Kenneth Feinberg, the administrator or “Special Master” of the Fund, ignores the specifics regarding the non-economic losses written into the law by Congress. Feinberg set these damages at about 1/10th what they would be in a similar contemporary court case.

 

To understand how the award is computed, two factors are used:

 

             A.    “Economic losses” are computed by figuring how much money the victim would have earned over the rest of his/her working life, then subtracting what it would have cost that person to live. What is left over is called “economic losses.”

 

             B.    “Non-economic losses” means losses for physical and emotional pain, suffering, physical impairment, mental anguish, disfigurement, loss of companionship, loss of enjoyment of life, etc. Mr. Feinberg set the non-economic losses at $250,000 for the victim and $100,000 for a spouse and $100,000 for each child. (But remember, the Social Security deduction, mentioned earlier.) These figures are far below what is customarily awarded in court.


The Victims of the September 11 attacks deserve the same rights as every other American citizen. If, in the national interest, our individual rights to sue needed to be restricted, then the substitute compensation program must use the same formulas that would be used in court.



What is the Ulterior Motive?


On top of being unfair, there is an ulterior motive at work here. The Victim Compensation Fund is to be used to affect future lawsuits; it is being used to bring about tort (which means a lawsuit) reform through precedent. Here is how it will work.


Let’s say, two years down the line there is a product liability lawsuit; someone has been badly injured or even killed. The plaintiff is asking for two million dollars in pain and suffering. The defense attorney will get up in front of the jury and say, “the Victims of September 11 only got $250,000, why should this man get any more? Surely he didn’t suffer any more than those poor people who died in the Towers.”


And there you have it, a very powerful tool to change the course of future rewards in lawsuits.


Do we need tort reform? Yes. The medical industry is being ruined with lawsuits.


But you don’t sneak it in the back door and you don’t do it on our backs, the Victims’ Families.


It is IMMORAL to use us as a tool to achieve a political goal and we will not stand for it.


Fix the Fund’s Goal:


Congress shall pass, and the President shall sign, House bill (HR 3665) by Election Day 2002 which:

 

      1.   Repeals the section of the original law that specifies the deduction of insurance, etc.

      2.   Specifies a minimum amount for non-economic damages of $1,000,000 per family.


$1,000,000 sounds like a lot of money, and it is if you go out and spend it all. But if you invest it appropriately to generate income, it will only give you about $50,000 income. That doesn’t go very far in the New York City or the Washington, D.C. areas due to the high cost of living.
